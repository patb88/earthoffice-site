That’s actually a very insightful and well-phrased question — and yes, psychologists have identified versions of what you’re describing. The behavior you’re pointing to sits at the intersection of avoidance, displacement, and sometimes partial approach coping.

Let’s unpack it a bit:

1. Substitute or “Safety” Behaviors in Avoidance

In avoidant coping, people often engage in symbolic or proximal activities related to the avoided task as a way of reducing anxiety without confronting the full trigger.

  • For example, someone afraid to send an email to a supervisor might spend time re-writing drafts endlessly or reading articles about workplace communication instead.
  • These are called “safety behaviors” or “ritualized substitutes” in cognitive-behavioral therapy. They look like progress, but they primarily serve to reduce anxiety rather than resolve the core issue.
  • In exposure-based models, this is a form of covert avoidance — approaching “just enough” to feel active, but not enough to risk the feared outcome.

2. Displacement and Pseudo-Mastery

From a psychodynamic angle, this can resemble displacement: energy that should go toward the avoided action is redirected to a “nearby” behavior that feels safer or more controllable.

  • Freud described this as substituting a “compromise formation” — a behavior that symbolically addresses the conflict but keeps the real fear unchallenged.
  • Modern therapists might frame this as pseudo-mastery: gaining a sense of agency through adjacent activity while maintaining emotional safety.

3. Partial Approach and the Illusion of Progress

Some contemporary cognitive models (like approach–avoidance conflict theory) note that avoidance isn’t binary; people often oscillate between the poles.

  • Doing something “in the general direction” can relieve the approach–avoidance tension temporarily, even though it doesn’t advance the goal.
  • Over time, this can reinforce the avoidance loop because the person experiences anxiety relief without actual exposure — a classic negative reinforcement cycle.

4. Terms You Might Look Up

Depending on the theoretical lens:

  • Cognitive-behavioral: “covert avoidance,” “safety behavior,” “partial approach coping”
  • Psychodynamic: “displacement,” “compromise formation,” “pseudo-mastery”
  • Behavioral economics / self-regulation: “substitution behavior,” “non-functional task displacement,” or colloquially, “productive procrastination”
