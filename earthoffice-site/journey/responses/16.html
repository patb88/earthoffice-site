Below is a clear, structured, and conversational explanation of the phases of the typical female menstrual cycle. I’ll keep it biologically precise but easy to internalize.

To orient you: the menstrual cycle is usually described in three coordinated layers that run in parallel:

  1. Ovarian cycle (follicular → ovulation → luteal)
  2. Endometrial cycle (menstruation → proliferative → secretory)
  3. Hormonal cycle (FSH, LH, estrogen, progesterone dynamics)

Most people only ever hear about one or two layers, but all three matter.

1. Follicular Phase (Ovaries)

Timeframe: Day 1 to roughly Day 14
Dominant hormone: Estrogen (estradiol)
Key brain hormones: FSH rising → LH rises later

What’s happening

  • Day 1 is defined as the first day of bleeding.
  • The brain (pituitary) releases FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone).
  • This stimulates several follicles in the ovary to begin maturing, each holding one egg.
  • One follicle becomes dominant; it sharply increases estradiol production.
  • Estrogen causes the uterine lining to rebuild (the proliferative phase).

What it tends to feel like

  • Often the “clearer, lighter, more energetic” part of the month.
  • Rising estrogen improves mood, cognition, working memory, and libido.
  • Body temperature is low and stable.

2. Ovulation

Timeframe: Very short—approximately mid-cycle
Dominant hormone dynamic: Estrogen peaks → triggers LH surge
Event: Release of the mature egg

What’s happening

  • When estrogen reaches a threshold, it flips from inhibitory to stimulatory, triggering a sharp LH surge.
  • LH causes the follicle to release the egg → ovulation.

What it tends to feel like

  • Sometimes a one-sided twinge (mittelschmerz).
  • LH and estrogen peaks can create increased sexual desire, social assertiveness, and verbal fluency.
  • Cervical mucus becomes stretchy and egg-white-like.

3. Luteal Phase (Ovaries)

Timeframe: Ovulation to the start of the next period (~14 days)
Dominant hormone: Progesterone (with moderate estrogen)
Key hormonal pattern: Progesterone rises → stays high → falls sharply if no pregnancy

What’s happening

  • The ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum, a progesterone-secreting gland.
  • Progesterone stabilizes and “matures” the uterine lining (the secretory phase).
  • If fertilization does not occur, the corpus luteum dies → progesterone crashes → menstruation begins.

What it tends to feel like

  • First half of luteal: grounded, calmer, sometimes sleepier; body temp rises ~0.3–0.5°C due to progesterone.
  • Second half of luteal: PMS effects for many individuals—
    • irritability
    • emotional sensitivity
    • breast tenderness
    • bloating
    • lower energy
  • These effects are from falling progesterone + falling estrogen.

4. Menstruation (Uterine Cycle Reset)

Timeframe: Day 1–5 on average
Hormonal state: Both estrogen and progesterone at baseline (low)

What’s happening

  • The uterine lining is shed because progesterone dropped below the threshold needed to maintain it.
  • FSH begins rising again, which restarts the follicular phase.

What it tends to feel like

  • Cramping from prostaglandins.
  • Fatigue due to low hormones.
  • Often a sense of emotional “reset.”

Mid-cycle: Ovulation (LH Surge → Egg Release)
Days 14–28: Luteal (Progesterone ↑ then ↓)
Next Day 1: Menstruation (Hormones reset)

Yes—that’s exactly right. Menstruation and the follicular phase do overlap for roughly the first 4–6 days of the cycle.

Here’s the clean way to think about it:

  • Day 1 of bleeding = Day 1 of the follicular phase.
  • Menstruation is technically the first sub-phase of the follicular phase in the ovarian cycle framework, even though many people treat them as separate categories because they feel so different.

Why they overlap

The menstrual bleed happens because progesterone has crashed at the end of the luteal phase.
The moment progesterone drops low enough, the uterine lining destabilizes and begins shedding.

At the same time, the pituitary begins releasing FSH, which starts maturing a new set of follicles in the ovary. That’s the beginning of the follicular phase.

So you have two simultaneous processes:

  • Uterus: clearing out the old lining
  • Ovaries/brain: gearing up the follicles for the new cycle

Typical timeline

  • Day 1–5: Menstruation (part of early follicular phase)
  • Day 1–14: Follicular phase overall
  • Day 6–14: “Late follicular” (estrogen rising sharply, one follicle becomes dominant)

Endocrine logic

This “overlap” is simply because the ovarian cycle (follicular → ovulation → luteal) and the uterine cycle (menstruation → proliferative → secretory) are running on parallel clocks driven by the same endocrine signals.
