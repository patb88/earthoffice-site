This question persists because each of the common answers captures a real slice of the truth, but none of them is sufficient on its own. The behavior you are pointing at is multi-layered, context-dependent, and shaped by different incentive systems operating simultaneously.

I’ll lay out a clear framework that reconciles the apparent contradictions rather than choosing a single winner.

Short answer (executive summary)

Women do not dress for a single audience.

They dress across three overlapping domains:

  1. Intra-female social signaling (status, taste, belonging)
  2. Self-regulation and embodiment (how it feels to inhabit the body)
  3. Situational male attention (when relevant, constrained, or instrumental)

Which domain dominates depends on context, age, safety, culture, and power asymmetry.

Layer 1: Women dressing for other women (the strongest claim)

This is the most structurally accurate statement in most modern social environments.

Why it’s true

Women exist in dense social networks where peer evaluation matters constantly:

  • Taste
  • Competence
  • Status
  • Cultural fluency
  • Belonging vs. exclusion

Clothing operates as a high-bandwidth social signal inside these networks.

Importantly:

  • Women are far better at decoding other women’s fashion signals than men.
  • Subtlety matters (fit, fabric, restraint, coherence).
  • The audience is continuous and unavoidable (workplaces, social groups, public spaces).

Example

A man may register “attractive / not attractive.”
Another woman registers:

  • effort vs. ease
  • trend awareness vs. desperation
  • confidence vs. insecurity
  • socioeconomic cues
  • intentionality

That is a different level of scrutiny entirely.

This is why statements like “men don’t even notice” are often correct. The signal was never meant for them.

Layer 2: Women dressing for themselves (also true, but misunderstood)

This phrase is often dismissed as platitudinal, but it has a real psychological meaning when framed correctly.

Women dress to regulate:

  • mood
  • confidence
  • sense of coherence
  • bodily ownership
  • identity continuity

This is not abstract self-expression; it is embodied cognition.

Clothing feeds back into:

  • posture
  • movement
  • self-perception
  • behavioral confidence

This is why:

  • “power dressing” works
  • comfort can feel like safety
  • certain outfits feel right even in isolation

This layer operates even when no one is watching.

Layer 3: Dressing for men (situational, not dominant)

This is the most controversial layer because it is both overclaimed and underacknowledged.

When it matters

  • Dating contexts
  • Mating competition
  • Explicit romantic signaling
  • Certain nightlife or social rituals
  • Situations where male attention has material consequences

In these contexts, yes—women absolutely dress with men in mind.

But:

  • This is contextual, not universal
  • It is often strategic, not submissive
  • It is frequently constrained by safety considerations

Crucially, this layer is less continuous than intra-female signaling. You can exit it; you cannot exit female peer visibility.

Why the debate feels polarized

The arguments usually collapse because people implicitly assume a single audience.

Men often assume:

“If it looks attractive to me, it must be for me.”

Women often respond:

“You are not the primary audience.”

Both statements can be true simultaneously.

The conflict arises from audience misattribution, not dishonesty.

One subtle but important point

Women are historically and socially trained to manage perception.

That does not mean they are passive or performative.
It means they are skilled at simultaneous signaling:

  • to others
  • to themselves
  • to the environment

Men, on average, operate with fewer overlapping perceptual demands. This difference alone explains much of the confusion.

Bottom line

Women do not dress for one group.

They dress within a social ecology, optimizing for:

  • peer legitimacy
  • internal alignment
  • situational desire
  • safety
  • autonomy

When someone says:

  • “Women dress for men” → contextually true

The mistake is treating any of these as exclusive.
