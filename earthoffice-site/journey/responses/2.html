2. Why make so much total T if most is bound?

Nature’s logic here is subtle — testosterone acts more like a distributed resource than a simple “use it or lose it” fuel:

  • Bound testosterone = reservoir.
    • Think of SHBG as a transport truck, carrying testosterone around the bloodstream.
    • When tissues need it, some unbinds (especially from albumin, which binds loosely).
    • This keeps hormone levels steady instead of spiking wildly minute to minute.
  • Buffering system.
    • Hormones work best in narrow ranges; binding proteins buffer fluctuations so receptors aren’t constantly overloaded.
  • Cross-talk with other hormones.
    • The same SHBG also binds estradiol, keeping a consistent estrogen/androgen balance available to tissues.
    • The liver adjusts SHBG levels depending on metabolic cues (thyroid, insulin, estrogen, etc.) — a built-in fine-tuner.
  • Evolutionary context.
    • In younger adults with lower SHBG, more free T circulates for growth, muscle, reproduction.
    • As we age, SHBG rises — less free T means lower reproductive drive and anabolic pressure, which was adaptive for energy conservation and longevity in post-reproductive years.

So the body’s making “too much” total T only from a modern lab perspective; biologically, it’s maintaining a large circulating buffer and tight receptor control, not wasting energy.
