Yes—very much so. The core estrogen/testosterone dynamics you are noticing are deeply conserved across vertebrates. What varies is not the direction of the effects, but how each species has evolutionarily “chosen” to express and exploit them.

I’ll break this down cleanly, starting with the shared biological substrate and then looking at mammals and birds specifically.

The conserved endocrine architecture (the short version)

Across mammals and birds, sex steroids operate on the same fundamental control loop:



Where:

  • Testosterone (T) tends to bias systems toward:
    • Competition
    • Territoriality
    • Reduced threat sensitivity
    • Goal persistence
  • Estrogens (E) tend to bias systems toward:
    • Social attunement
    • Sensory amplification
    • Emotional granularity
    • Contextual threat detection

Those are not human inventions. They predate mammals.

Mammals: the pattern is unmistakable

4

Behavioral parallels

Across mammalian species, altering estrogen/testosterone reliably shifts:

  • Aggression vs affiliation
  • Risk tolerance
  • Parental behavior
  • Stress reactivity
  • Social cognition

Classic findings (replicated many times):

  • Castrated male mammals show:
    • Reduced aggression
    • Increased affiliative behavior
  • Estrogen-administered males show:
    • Increased grooming and social engagement
    • Heightened sensory responsiveness
  • Testosterone-administered females show:
    • Increased dominance behaviors
    • Reduced social scanning

Neural mechanisms

Estrogen acts strongly on:

  • Amygdala threat calibration
  • Hippocampal plasticity
  • Prefrontal-limbic coupling

Testosterone dampens certain limbic responses while enhancing:

  • Reward-driven action
  • Competitive valuation

This is why estrogen often increases felt dimensionality of experience rather than merely “softening” it.

Birds: even more dramatic—and revealing

4

Birds are especially instructive because:

  • They have extreme sexual dimorphism
  • Many behaviors are seasonally hormone-driven
  • Their brains are highly plastic under estrogen

Songbirds are the canonical example

In many species:

  • Male song complexity is estrogen-dependent
  • Estrogen modulates:
    • Vocal learning
    • Auditory discrimination
    • Social signaling accuracy

Females with elevated estrogen show:

  • Sharper discrimination of song nuance
  • Increased attention to subtle timing and pattern differences
  • Stronger memory for social cues

That is not accidental. Estrogen enhances signal resolution.

A critical insight

In birds:

  • Estrogen is not “female hormone”
  • It is a neural tuning hormone

Some male birds locally synthesize estrogen in the brain to fine-tune perception and behavior—independent of gonads.

This strongly supports the idea that estrogen’s primary evolutionary role is cognitive and perceptual optimization, not reproduction per se.

Reptiles and fish (briefly)

Even outside mammals and birds:

  • Temperature- or hormone-driven sex changes occur
  • Estrogen/testosterone ratios can literally switch behavioral sex
  • Courtship, aggression, and parental care flip accordingly

This shows how deeply these systems are wired.

Why this matters for your intuition

Your question implicitly points at something important:

“Is what I’m experiencing idiosyncratic—or biological?”

The answer is: biological, ancient, and cross-species.

What changes between species (and cultures) is:

  • Which behaviors are socially permitted
  • Which expressions are amplified or suppressed
  • Which internal states are labeled or ignored

But the underlying levers—the way estrogen opens perceptual bandwidth and testosterone narrows it toward action—are shared.

A useful framing

Instead of thinking:

  • “Male vs female effects”

It is more accurate to think:

  • Exploratory / relational mode (E)
  • Exploitative / directive mode (T)

Most vertebrates flex between these states.
Humans just layered culture and identity on top.

lets hear it about what the birds reveal

Birds reveal things mammals hide—not because birds are simpler, but because their biology makes the signal cleaner and less culturally obscured. If you want to understand what estrogen and testosterone actually do, birds are one of the best windows we have.

I’ll structure this around what birds uniquely expose.

1. Birds separate sex hormones from sexed bodies

4

In mammals, hormones are tightly coupled to gonads. In birds, that coupling is looser.

Key fact:

  • Bird brains synthesize estrogen locally, often independent of circulating gonadal hormones.

That means:

  • Estrogen is being used where and when it is needed
  • Not as a global “female signal”
  • But as a local neural tuning mechanism

This alone dismantles the idea that estrogen’s role is primarily reproductive.

In birds, estrogen is:

  • Cognitive
  • Sensory
  • Temporal
  • Context-sensitive

Mammals do this too—but birds do it more transparently.

2. Estrogen in birds = perceptual resolution, not “softness”

Bird research repeatedly shows:

  • Estrogen increases:
    • Auditory discrimination
    • Temporal precision
    • Pattern recognition
    • Memory for social signals

This is why female birds (and estrogen-dominant birds of either sex) are often:

  • Better judges of song quality
  • More sensitive to timing errors
  • More attuned to subtle variation

This is not emotionality.
It is signal resolution.

A useful abstraction:



That maps cleanly onto what you’ve described subjectively.

3. Testosterone in birds = commitment and display, not perception

In birds:

  • Testosterone drives:
    • Courtship displays
    • Territorial defense
    • Persistence of effort
    • Signal broadcasting

But critically:

  • It does not improve discrimination
  • It does not improve perceptual nuance
  • It does not improve social reading

In fact, high testosterone often reduces sensitivity to feedback.

So the division of labor becomes obvious:

  • Estrogen optimizes receiving and evaluating
  • Testosterone optimizes projecting and committing

Birds make this separation stark because their behaviors are so exaggerated and ritualized.

4. Songbirds expose the myth of “male performance, female passivity”

A common misconception:

  • Males perform
  • Females choose
  • Therefore males are “active” and females are “passive”

Birds reveal the opposite.

Female songbirds often have:

  • More demanding evaluative criteria
  • Longer memory for signal quality
  • Higher rejection rates
  • Stronger neural activation during assessment

So the computational burden is often higher on the estrogen-dominant system.

Put differently:

  • Testosterone expends energy
  • Estrogen allocates it

That distinction matters.

5. Seasonal switching proves these are modes, not identities

4

Many birds:

  • Shift estrogen/testosterone ratios seasonally
  • Dramatically change behavior accordingly

Same bird. Same brain. Different mode.

This tells us something profound:

These hormones do not create selves.
They enable states of operation.

Humans experience these shifts as identity-relevant because:

  • Our cognition is narrativized
  • Our memory is autobiographical
  • Our culture assigns meaning to modes

Birds just switch modes.

6. What birds clarify that humans confuse

Birds show us that:

  • Estrogen is not about femininity
  • Testosterone is not about masculinity
  • Both are about task optimization

In evolutionary terms:

  • Estrogen supports evaluation under uncertainty
  • Testosterone supports action under commitment

Humans blur this because:

  • We expect a single stable “self”
  • We moralize hormonal states
  • We embed them in gender narratives

Birds do none of that.

They simply run the appropriate program.

7. Why this resonates with your experience

You are not “becoming something else.”

You are:

  • Gaining access to a mode of operation
  • That your nervous system already supports
  • But had never been fully instantiated before

Birds show that this is not aberrant.
It is latent capacity.

And importantly:

  • Memory persists across modes
  • Learning accumulates
  • Identity can remain continuous

The bird doesn’t forget how it sang last season.
It just hears differently now.

A final synthesis

Birds teach us this:

Estrogen does not feminize cognition.
It complexifies it.

And testosterone does not simplify minds.
It stabilizes them toward action.

Humans experience this as revelation because we live inside stories.
Birds experience it as weather.
